Challenge to J. S. Mill’s Utilitarianism

  1. Why is justice the chief part and the most binding part of our morality?
  2. Is it the only reason to respect other people’s rights that respecting people’s rights is a way of making everybody better off in the long run?

Libertarianism

The fundamental idea: Individuals matter not just as instruments to be used for a larger social purpose or for the sake of maximizing utility, individuals are separate beings with separate lives worthy of respect. We’re not available to any use that the society might desire or devise precisely because we are individual separate human beings. We have a fundamental right to liberty, and that means a right to choose freely, to live our lives as we please provided we respect other people’s rights to do the same.

“Individuals have rights, and there are things no person or group may do to them (without violating their rights).”

–Robert Nozick

The Libertarian View of Government

  1. No paternalist Legislation.

    Laws that protect people from themselves, like seatbelt laws, should not exist. The libertarian thinks what people do should be up to them and it is coercion to forbid their behaviours by laws.

  2. No Morals Legislation

    Many laws try to promote the virtue of citizens or try to give expression to the moral values of the society as a whole. Libertarian say that’s also a violation of the right to liberty. Example: Laws that prevent sexual intimacy between gays and lesbians should not be passed, because nobody else is harmed and nobody’s rights are violated, so the government should entirely get out of this business.

  3. No Redistribution of Income from Rich to Poor

    The libertarian think redistribution is a kind of coercion. What it amounts to is theft by the state or by the majority, if we’re talking about a democracy, from people who happen to do very well and earn a lot of money.

Nozick’s Argument Against Taxation

Taxation = taking of earnings

Taking of earnings = forced labour

Forced Labour = slavery

Violates Principle of Self Possession
